15 Most common Scams or Scams in the NFT world

ByInfobloom

May 3, 2022

In the NFT and crypto world, the price to pay for owning our wallets is having to be alert and pay close attention to possible scams. In this post, we gather the most common so that you do not fall into them.

1. The DM on Discord

The DM on Discord is one of the most common scams to see: someone who looks like they belong to a project sends you a DM on Discord.

There is always a link to click on and it is most likely that if you do, there will be an exact copy of the project website and if you connect your wallet, you will lose everything. In this example, we see that the Opensea link ends in .fo instead of .io.

To prevent this, we may limit who sends us messages in this application, in the optionsclicking on Privacy.

Later, We deselect the option so that users of servers where we are can send us DMs. Of course, remember to activate it again for specific servers in which you are interested in doing so (usually the smallest and most private ones).

2. The fake email

The crypto world, being a new ecosystem, is seeing how scams that worked very well at the beginning of the internet are repeated. One of them is the phishing. The email you see below is legitimate and is from a purchase from lassofilms. However, many times we will receive emails like the one below from a domain like opensea .tl or the like.

Many times they will send us an email saying that one of our most valuable pieces sold for a small amount, unleashing panic in us and causing us to click on the link. These types of emails play on urgency and panic.

To avoid this, always try to go to the browser and log in from the link you know from the marketplace.

Here we can see another example where you were sent to luke leon an email purportedly from the Foundation. However, the domain ended with .art instead of .com

3. Discord Friend

Identity theft is also one of the most typical scams in the NFT world. Somebody copy the entire profile of a friend of yours who they know you have a relationship with. Tea they send a message and take advantage of your trust to ask you for money or some kind of private information.

In this example, we see how someone impersonated Matty (DCLblogger) to ask a friend of his to sell him BTC for ETH. We have also seen cases of Spanish-speaking friends who, for some “unknown reason”, speak to us in English and have no idea of ​​Spanish.

To avoid this, check very well the username of the person speaking to you on Discord and other social networks.

4.DM on Twitter

This type of scam mostly affects artists who sell their art as NFTs. A collector sends you a DM on Twitter saying that will buy your piece if you perform some type of action first (send cryptocurrencies or click a link).

Obviously, no collector will ask you to send them money to pay for gas fees or anything like that. Therefore, always be wary of these types of messages.

In this image we can see how a “collector” sent a message to Fran Rodriguez following this scam model.

5. Fake accounts on Twitter

Sometimes accounts are hacked with the “verified” symbol on Twitter and they change their name so that many people fall for a scam. One of the most typical that we have seen is to impersonate the account of Elon Musk or crypto exchanges. Even Elon Musk himself was hacked back in the day due to an internal Twitter error.

How can we realize that it is a scam?

  • Number of responses and low interaction: Although it is not totally indicative, if a tweet has very low interaction compared to other tweets, it could be because it is a scam.

6. Google Ads

In Google Ads you can write the link you want. In fact, you can perfectly make ads from any website, whether it is scam or not.

Many scammers buy very similar domains to those of the main platforms such as opensea. The original is Opensea.io, but you can see Opensea .tl, .lo, etc. If you click, you will see a website exactly like the one you expect to find, but it is a scam.

A while ago, there was a scam like this with meta mask that made a lot of people download a malicious software. This resulted in many hacked wallets.

To avoid this, try to never click on the ads and always look at the domain name and make sure it is what it should be.

7. Fake NFTs: copymining

One of the scams that most resembles those of the physical world is the copymining. This is generating a collection of NFTs that look like the real thing but are fake copies. They do this by writing a similar name in marketplaces like Opensea and using the original image to lie

This is something that, as he told us in the interview, happened to Pequelord several times. He recommended that, if you are an artist, you buy a domain (YourCollection.com) that goes directly to your collection in a marketplace so that people can buy from it.

In order not to fall into this type of scam as a buyer, you can investigate using Etherscan and seeing which wallet is behind the minteo or access directly from the profile links of the artist in question.

8. Exchange account hack

Although this is something that is perhaps more of the crypto world in general, it can perfectly affect the people of the NFT world.

Sometimes, the user account of certain exchanges has been hacked because it has been possible to access (through phishing or other methods) the email account and even the SMS of the phone number. This is possible because there is a black market that allows the purchase of duplicate SIM cards.

For this reason, there are times when neither the 2FA (2-factor authentication) can save us.

What we can do is have a cold wallet like Ledger or Trezor or, at a minimum, store our cryptocurrencies in our Metamask wallet.

9. Airdrops

In many cases, they create airdrops from fake accounts that invite you to log in to certain fake platforms with your Metamask wallet. Once we do, they will have access to our wallet and all its assets.

In this case, we can see how they made an account very similar to UniSwap with a lot of followers. However the actual handle of Uniswap is @Uniswap and the link is Uniswap.org. Here we can see that none of these things are fulfilled in the image.

10. Airdrop 2.0

There is a lot of other version more dangerous from airdrops, since it seems harmless and can make us lose everything.

Basically, there are ERC-20 tokens (“cryptocurrencies”) in Ethereum and other blockchains, which can empty our wallets.

The way they deceive us is: we see our wallet, we see that we have received a token and that it also has a great value ($100,000) and by going to decentralized exchanges like Uniswap and signing the smart contract to be able to operate with these tokens and sell them , you give permission to empty your wallet.

Be very careful with the tokens that we do not know!

11. Coldwallet hacked

Sometimes when you buy your coldwallet like Ledger or Trezor from an unofficial provider (Amazon or eBay), you may actually be getting a hacked edition.

They can do this in several ways and it is not always the seller’s fault.

For example, someone may buy a coldwallet on amazon, hack it and return it. The seller, seeing that it is only a return, resells it to another customer.

It may also be the case that you see a great deal because the physical wallet carries malicious software inside.

Always buy your device from the official provider.

12. Fake lies

Something that gave a lot to talk about in the ecosystem is the false mints. This is, make an NFT appear as if it was minted from someone famous’s wallet like Beeple (at least for platforms like Opensea).

This is possible because you can create a NFT automatically sent to someone’s wallet and come back without your permission. Opensea interprets that, as it was the first wallet it went through, it was that person who lied to it, giving rise to misunderstandings.

To avoid this, we can see in etherscan the history of this NFT.

13. Fake Collaboration

Something that is also given a lot and that has been a reason why several artists have lost a lot of money are the fake collaborations.

This consists of someone, from a company apparently with a following and a reputation, talking to you because they would like you to do art for an NFT that they are going to release. Although it may seem legitimate at first, they send you a “sample” file of what they want and it is actually a file that will make you lose everything. In this image of Xavier Iglesiaswe see how they tried to deceive him.

To avoid these types of scams, try never ever download sample files. For example, you can display them within any platform like Google Drive or directly on Youtube. They usually come tablets on purpose so you have to download them to your computer.

14. Free and dangerous NFTs

The last of the scams, although not much is known about it. The rumor has been spreading that there are NFTs that send you to your wallet and that if you interact with them, they can get your wallet hacked.

Although it is not known to what extent this may be so, it is best to avoid finding out and do not interact with the NFT at Opensea. Sometimes, we will see that the NFT in question has been listed for a fortune and we will be tempted to sell it. But, just like airdrop 2.0, this is just another tactic to trick you.

Normally they will be minted in the Polygon blockchain or similarsince it is much cheaper to send NFTs in bulk.

In the image, we see on the left a “classic kitty“, an NFT that several people receive and that we do not know who is behind it. For this reason, we hide it, but it seems that Opensea ended up blocking it from the marketplace.

15. False price in Opensea

A scam that was around for a while and still has its victims is making a offer someone at Opensea in a cryptocurrency of much less value, making the person believe that it is ETH. Although Opensea has taken measures to make this not so easy, some time ago, the scammer in question put on the ETH token of profile image and thus managed to deceive the seller, who due to the rush and emotion of the sale, accepted the offer upon seeing the number (ex: 15) next to symbol.

Final note

As we can see, many of these scams use the social engineering so that you click on a link and log in with your wallet where you shouldn’t. To avoid falling into it, always try don’t download files or click on links and don’t panic if you see a message about something that shouldn’t happen.
